Draft Vision Statement

What We Are Building Toward

All Texas students are prepared to thrive personally and professionally in an AI-driven world — equipped with the critical thinking, ethical reasoning, and technical understanding needed to use AI effectively, evaluate it skeptically, and shape it responsibly.

"We really want every Texas student to be prepared to thrive both personally and professionally in an AI-driven world. Right now, Texas is not being strategic about how we reach that vision."

— Carol Fletcher, Director, EPIC / TACC

This vision demands action across six interconnected areas, each identified as a gap or priority by task force members:

What This Vision Requires

  • State leadership — TEA must have an empowered point person and a published AI strategy
  • Updated standards — TEKS must explicitly name AI literacy K–12 with grade-level specificity
  • Educator preparation — Pre- and in-service training must include AI literacy for teachers, counselors, and administrators
  • District guidance — Model policies, procurement criteria, and tool-vetting frameworks
  • Student well-being — Psychological, social, and emotional impacts addressed proactively
  • Equity — AI literacy opportunities reaching every student regardless of zip code, income, or language

What This Vision Rejects

  • Banning or ignoring AI as though it does not exist in students' lives
  • Allowing AI to replace student thinking rather than augment it
  • Building policy around specific tools rather than durable frameworks
  • Leaving Texas in a reactive posture while 46 other states have issued formal AI guidance

A TASA survey of 322 respondents representing 277 districts revealed that most existing student AI policies (out of 43 analyzed) focus almost entirely on what not to do — cheating, academic integrity, data privacy — with almost no affirmative guidance.

The top administrator need identified: examples and guidance for writing AI policies (40%), followed by professional development and staff training (26%). Only 9% cited AI curriculum or tools as a need.

Top 3 Concerns

  2. Current AI Policies
  3. Student Data Privacy
Lowest-Ranked Concerns
  1. Uneven/Unfair Access
  2. Psychological Effects on Students
  3. Student Creativity
⚠️

Red Flag: Preparing students for the psychological, social, and emotional impacts of AI ranks near the bottom of administrator concerns — not because it doesn't matter, but because leaders are in triage mode over cheating and data privacy. The task force identified this gap as urgent.

What Exists in Texas Standards

K–12 Standards

  • K–8 Tech Apps: "emerging technologies" mentioned vaguely in creativity/innovation sections only
  • 3 high school CTE courses include "artificial intelligence" in the title (cybersecurity, game programming, advanced cloud computing)
  • Core CS courses (CS 1, 2, 3): AI is not mentioned in standards at all
  • CS teacher certification: AI mentioned once in competencies

The Opportunity

  • 1.5 million CTE students enrolled in 2024–25 — inserting AI literacy into CTE employability/preamble standards could reach far more students than current pathways
  • 128 TEKS exist for Tech Apps, but only vaguely address emerging technologies — a revision is feasible and overdue
  • The Public Education Committee of the Texas Legislature has been charged with reviewing AI in classrooms — the window for action is open
What Is AI Literacy?

A Framework for Texas

"We can't let AI replace thinking. But equally dangerous is acting as though AI doesn't exist. And we can't make it a conversation about who has the shiniest tool."

— Alan Antoine, Director of CS Education Strategy, TACC/EPIC

AI literacy is defined as the practical, technical, and ethical understanding of AI. Across leading frameworks — Where We Teach AI, DataCamp, Digital Promise, TeachAI — four elements consistently appear:

🔍

Teaching About AI

How AI works — its mechanics, limitations, and design. Students who understand the system can evaluate it critically.

🤝

Teaching With AI

Integrating AI into daily practice as a tool — learning to collaborate with, not defer to, AI systems.

⚙️

Creating With AI

Applied, generative use — building, designing, and producing with AI as an active co-creator.

⚖️

Ethics Woven Throughout

The ethical thread runs through all three layers — bias, privacy, accountability, human dignity, and the right to think for oneself.

Why Computer Science has a unique role:

CS can teach how AI actually works, enabling students to understand both its power and its limits. Understanding the mechanics leads naturally to ethical reasoning. As one task force member put it: "I want my kids to learn AI well enough that they don't trust it for anything."

The future-of-work framing offered by AI researchers at the meeting: AI use exists on a spectrum between delegating the task and delegating the role. Neither extreme is coming — the TBD bell curve requires critical thinking and effective human-machine collaboration skills. The evergreen focus: critical thinking + ability to collaborate with AI systems.

AI Policy & Strategy

TeachAI Policy Framework

Foundational Policy Ideas for AI in Education

Published April 2024 by TeachAI — an initiative led by Code.org, ETS, ISTE, Khan Academy, and the World Economic Forum — and informed by over 70 organizations. This resource offers policy briefs, talking points, and a customizable presentation built around five foundational policy ideas:

  1. Foster Leadership — Establish an AI in Education Task Force to oversee policy development
  2. Promote AI Literacy — Integrate AI concepts and skills into existing curriculum and instruction
  3. Provide Guidance — Equip schools with guidance on safe and responsible AI use
  4. Build Capacity — Provide funding and programs to support educator professional development
  5. Support Innovation — Promote research and development of safe, effective AI in education

The resource includes a Policy Tracker with maps and links to AI guidance from states and countries globally, and a landscape analysis of emerging guidance themes. It is the most comprehensive policy scaffolding resource available to Texas as it begins its own strategy development.

EDSAFE AI Alliance Student Safety

S.A.F.E. by Design: Policy, Research, and Practice Recommendations for AI Companions in Education

Published February 2026 by the EDSAFE AI Alliance — a global workgroup of over 70 educators, technologists, policymakers, researchers, and youth advocates. This report directly addresses the task force's lowest-ranked concern (student psychological well-being) and makes that gap impossible to ignore.

Key findings informing Texas policy:

  • About 1 in 3 students reports that conversations with AI companions are as satisfying (or more satisfying) than interactions with real-life friends
  • More than 70% of teens have used AI chatbots or purpose-built companions at least once; over half use them monthly
  • A documented link exists between AI companion interactions and tragic instances of youth harm — the report frames this as a life-and-death policy issue
  • Approximately 31% of high school students have used AI chatbots for emotional support
  • The report warns against AI design features that mimic social media engagement mechanics and calls for "Developmentally Aligned Design" that respects the cognitive and emotional limitations of childhood

The S.A.F.E. framework stands for: Safe, Accountable, Fair, and Effective. The report includes actionable K–12 recommendations, design guidelines rooted in learning science, a district chatbot safety checklist, and a research roadmap. It argues that state education agencies need at minimum a dedicated AI point person — directly echoing this task force's own recommendations.

Defining AI Literacy for K–12

While defining AI literacy or specific learning standards is not a goal of this Task Force, these two research-grounded resources provide essential context for understanding what AI literacy means for students — and what Texas would be building toward.

CSTA + AI4K12 Standards

AI Learning Priorities for All K–12 Students

Published 2025 by the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA) in partnership with AI4K12. This project convened teachers, researchers, administrators, and curriculum developers to identify AI learning priorities for revised K–12 CS standards, directly informing the 2026 CSTA K–12 Standards.

The project identified five categories for AI learning across all grade bands:

  1. Humans and AI
  2. Representation and Reasoning
  3. Machine Learning
  4. Ethical AI System Design and Programming
  5. Societal Impacts of AI

Because the full scope of AI content exceeds what can be covered in existing K–12 CS courses, the project emphasized the need to define priority levels — with the most important foundational content for all students marked clearly by grade band, regardless of future career choice. This is directly applicable to Texas's TEKS revision process.

Access Resource →
OECD · EC · Code.org International Framework

AI Literacy Framework: Empowering Learners for the Age of AI

Released May 2025 by the OECD and European Commission, with support from Code.org and international experts. This framework defines the knowledge, skills, and attitudes students need to engage with AI confidently, critically, and responsibly — and will contribute to the PISA 2029 Media and AI Literacy assessment.

Organized into four domains and 22 competencies:

  • Engage with AI — understanding what AI is, how it works, and how to use it
  • Create with AI — applying AI for problem-solving, communication, and creativity
  • Manage AI — evaluating outputs critically, identifying bias, making ethical judgments
  • Design AI — understanding how AI systems are built and shaped

AI literacy is defined across three dimensions: Knowledge, Skills, and Attitudes. The graphic on page 17 of the report provides a particularly useful visual for understanding these multiple dimensions — recommended as a reference point for Texas standards and PD design.

How Teens Use and View AI — Pew Research Center

The nonpartisan Pew Research Center surveyed 1,458 U.S. teens ages 13–17 and their parents (conducted September 25–October 9, 2025; published February 24, 2026) to produce this landmark report on how teens are actually using and thinking about AI. Key findings with direct implications for Texas:

64% of U.S. teens report ever using an AI chatbot
~30% use AI chatbots daily — some several times a day
54% have used AI to help with schoolwork
1 in 10 say AI helps with all or most of their schoolwork
12% have used chatbots for emotional support or advice
59% believe AI cheating happens at least somewhat often at their school

The Parent Awareness Gap

While 64% of teens report using AI chatbots, only 51% of parents believe their teen uses them — and about 30% of parents are unsure. Only 4 in 10 parents have talked to their teen about AI. This gap between what is happening and what adults believe is happening is precisely the problem task force members identified when calling for parent education and community engagement strategies.

Equity Dimensions

Black and Hispanic teens are more likely to use AI chatbots daily (35% and 33% respectively, vs. 22% of White teens) and more likely to report chatbots as extremely or very helpful for schoolwork. Texas's strategies must account for these different starting points — AI is already being adopted unevenly, and equity-first policy design is not optional.

📊

Texas Context: Texas's own data shows 61% of students are using AI — consistent with Pew's national findings. This report gives Texas policymakers a validated, demographically detailed picture of what is already happening in teens' lives. It directly supports the task force's argument that inaction is not a neutral choice: students are already in the AI landscape with or without adult guidance.

Breakout Sessions

Barriers & Challenges Identified

Task force members organized barriers across seven categories during structured breakout sessions. These were captured live via sticky notes and structured discussion.

Educator Preparation & Leadership
  • Educators arrive unprepared — no AI literacy in teacher prep programs
  • Training expected to replace genuine understanding
  • Lack of standards in educator preparation programs
  • Leaders lack the knowledge, skills, and mindset to support AI-integrated learning
  • Teachers need to know when not to use AI, not just how
Privacy & Safety
  • Student data (including IEP/504 documents) fed into unvetted AI systems
  • No clear district-level data boundaries for AI tools
  • FERPA/COPPA compliance obligations unclear for AI use cases
  • Tools that monitor student collaboration without consent
Strategy, Scale & Leadership
  • No single TEA point person empowered to lead AI education work
  • Multi-faceted challenge with no clear accountable leadership
  • Lack of institutional precedent at the state level
  • No practical mindset that supports cross-sector coordination
Attitudes About AI
  • Fear, resistance, and "AI guilt" among educators
  • Schools reverting to pen & paper as a response
  • Cultural differences in readiness and trust
  • Lack of knowledge and understanding about what AI actually does
Community Engagement & Values
  • Parents lack awareness of how AI is used in schools
  • No meaningful parental consultation processes in most districts
  • Generational differences in comfort and trust
  • Need for multilingual, culturally responsive outreach
Student Learning
  • Role of AI in digital literacy unclear — where does rigor live?
  • Curriculum alignment to AI not addressed in most districts
  • "Is it cheating?" — critical thinking and academic integrity in tension
  • Students and counselors already navigating emotional AI attachment
Equity & Access
  • AI literacy concentrated in well-resourced districts
  • Rural, low-income, and ELL students largely unserved
  • Insufficient investment in building capacity across the state
  • Implementation risks becoming an unfunded mandate

Task Force Recommendations

Solutions & Recommendations

The following recommendations were generated by task force members at the March 26 kickoff and recorded via structured sticky-note activities. They are organized by category and represent the collective consensus of the group.

Student Standards TEKS & AI Literacy

  • Update TEKS to explicitly incorporate AI literacy at grade-level resolution across K–12
  • Expand Computational Thinking standards to include how AI works
  • Require Technology Applications integrated across all subjects through 7th grade
  • Adopt grade-level-appropriate AI definitions and standards by grade band
  • Revise tech app standards to explicitly name AI (not just "emerging technologies")
  • Create a required course covering social media and emerging technologies

Training Educator Preparation & PD

  • Mandatory AI literacy training for all teachers, counselors, paras, and administrators
  • AI literacy standards of practice for counselors, teachers, and administrators
  • Pre-service teacher preparation must include AI literacy at all EPPs
  • Add a dedicated professional development day per year focused on AI training
  • Create tiered PD pathway with "AI Ambassador" designation to scale and empower educators
  • Incentive-based PD — rewards for early completion, not just mandates
  • State strategy to build educator capacity for teaching both with and about AI
  • Develop AI literacy frameworks, PD, and field-ready tools from the field — NOT just top-down from TEA
  • Reintegrate logic and intentional pedagogy into teacher instruction
  • Identify content knowledge and skills teachers need to supercharge student learning with AI

Policy Procurement & District Guidance

  • State guidance with model AI policy examples for both student and staff use
  • Publish district vignettes of how early-adopter districts have planned strategically
  • Create a vetting process and research base for AI tools deployed in classrooms
  • Establish baseline safety practices including student data minimization and wiping protocols
  • Update teacher code of conduct for the AI era
  • Develop technical assistance for school leaders to build local governance frameworks
  • Encourage procurement of tools that coach vs. answer — supporting student learning actively
  • Effective change management strategies for education leaders

Leadership Partnerships & State Infrastructure

  • TEA must designate an empowered point person to lead AI education work statewide
  • Form a state-wide steering committee of industry experts to advise TEA
  • Cross-sector, cross-district steering committees to break down silos
  • Provide dedicated funding from the state for a Texas strategic plan with priorities, targets, and timelines
  • Gather industry-defined core competencies to inform future-of-work preparation
  • Build educator-industry partnerships

Family Parent & Community Engagement

  • AI literacy training designed for parents and families
  • Help parents understand when and why AI will be taught in schools
  • Public information and awareness campaigns for all stakeholders
  • Town halls for understanding, training, and informing about AI
  • Use Title III bilingual family meeting strategies as a model for AI parent education

Research Evidence Base

  • Research initiatives to collect data and build evidence base for AI's impact on education
  • Dedicated funding to understand the psychological effects of AI use on students
  • Rubrics for educators to assess AI tools for usage, data collection, and safety

Strategic Approach Principles

Framework-based, not tool-based Incentive-driven, not mandate-only Multi-tiered: state → district → campus → teacher AI infused across all subjects K–12 and higher ed aligned Prepare for what's coming (agentic AI) Think in 3-year revision cycles Always ask: Does AI add value to the learning objective?
AI Readiness Rubric for Texas

Eight-Domain Readiness Rubric

This rubric translates the task force vision into observable indicators across eight domains. Score each domain 1–4. A score of 3 (Proficient) represents the minimum acceptable standard. A score of 4 (Exemplary) reflects what leading states and districts have achieved.

Prepared for April 13, 2026 review · Feedback deadline: April 20, 2026

Domain Key Indicator 1 — Beginning 2 — Developing 3 — Proficient 4 — Exemplary
1. State Vision & Leadership TEA has an articulated AI vision, designated point person, and published strategic plan. No state vision, no TEA AI lead, no plan in development. Vision drafted; TEA lead identified but lacks formal authority or resources. Vision published; TEA point person empowered; strategic plan underway. Vision widely adopted; TEA lead empowered with staff and budget; strategic plan active with measurable milestones.
2. Student Standards & AI Literacy K–12 TEKS explicitly address AI literacy in an age-appropriate, grade-banded, cross-curricular way. AI mentioned only vaguely (e.g., "emerging technologies"); no grade-level specificity. AI referenced in select CTE or CS courses; not integrated across grade bands or core subjects. TEKS updated to include AI literacy across K–12 with grade-level specificity; revision timeline published. AI literacy embedded across all K–12 TEKS; computational thinking and ethical use addressed at every grade band; revision cycle established.
3. Educator Preparation & PD Pre-service and in-service educators receive structured, sustained AI literacy training. No AI literacy requirements in EPPs or in-service PD; training ad hoc or absent. Some PD available; AI mentioned in EPP coursework; no state strategy or incentive structure. State strategy for educator AI capacity in place; EPPs include AI literacy; incentivized PD available to all educators. Mandatory AI literacy in all EPPs; tiered PD pathway (introductory to AI Ambassador); incentives tied to participation; counselors and admins included.
4. District Policy & Procurement Guidance TEA provides model AI policies, vetting criteria, and procurement guidance for districts. No state guidance; districts writing policies independently with no shared framework. Some guidance available but not systematically distributed; no vetting process for tools. TEA has published model AI policies and a tool vetting framework; districts can adapt to local context. Comprehensive guidance suite: model policies (student + staff), procurement criteria, early-adopter vignettes, updated teacher code of conduct.
5. Data Privacy & Student Safety Districts have clear FERPA/COPPA compliance guidance for AI tools and student data protection. No state guidance on AI-specific data privacy; districts uncertain about compliance. General FERPA/COPPA guidance exists but does not address AI tools specifically. AI-specific data privacy guidance published; vetting checklist addresses student PII; districts notified. Robust AI data safety framework: tool vetting, data minimization standards, student safety protocols, annual review cycle.
6. Social-Emotional Learning & Well-being AI's psychological, social, and emotional impacts on students are explicitly addressed in guidance and preparation. No mention of SEL or student well-being in any AI guidance; not a recognized priority. SEL impacts acknowledged in discussions; not yet embedded in standards or training. SEL dimensions of AI included in educator PD and counselor preparation; district guidance addresses student well-being. Comprehensive SEL + AI framework: counselor preparation, student mental health protocols, parent resources, research investment in AI's psychological impacts on youth.
7. Family & Community Engagement Parents and community members have access to AI literacy resources and are included in district AI planning. No parent-facing resources; families not engaged in AI policy or planning. Some informational events held; no systematic outreach or multilingual resources. Parent AI literacy resources available; town halls or community forums conducted; multilingual outreach underway. Sustained, multi-format family engagement: workshops, multilingual materials, parent AI literacy pathways, family input channels embedded in district planning.
8. Equity & Access AI literacy opportunities are equitably distributed across student populations, geographies, and school types. AI access concentrated in well-resourced districts; rural, low-income, and ELL students largely unserved. Equity acknowledged as a concern; no targeted strategies or resource allocations in place. Equity provisions embedded in state AI strategy; device/connectivity gaps addressed; underserved districts prioritized. Equity as a first-order design principle: targeted funding, device parity, culturally responsive AI curriculum, disaggregated outcome data reported publicly.

What Exemplary Looks Like

Scenarios: The Highest Order of What's Possible

These illustrative scenarios reflect Level 4 (Exemplary) implementation — grounded in the rubric domains, task force recommendations, and the vision articulated on March 26. They show what becomes possible when Texas acts decisively and strategically.

Domain 3 · Educator Preparation

The AI Ambassador Teacher

A 4th-grade bilingual teacher in El Paso completes her district's AI Ambassador pathway — a three-year scaffolded PD track certified by her EPP and aligned to revised TEKS. She now leads monthly learning communities for 18 colleagues, co-designing bilingual AI literacy lessons that use student questions about local water quality to explore how AI models find patterns in data. Her students learn to interrogate outputs, not just accept them.

Outcome: AI literacy reaches 400+ students in a historically underserved district — through teacher leadership, not a vendor contract.
Domain 6 · Social-Emotional Learning

The Counselor Who Was Ready

A high school counselor in Houston notices a student withdrawing from peers and describing an AI chatbot as their primary source of emotional support. Because her preparation program now includes an AI + SEL module, she recognizes the pattern, uses the state's student well-being protocol, and connects the student with a parent-counselor-teacher triad. The school's AI policy — developed with community input — has clear guidance for this scenario.

Outcome: Texas doesn't repeat the failure to anticipate social media's impact on youth. Counselors are prepared, not caught off guard.
Domain 7 · Family Engagement

The Parent Town Hall That Changed Everything

A mid-size district in the Rio Grande Valley hosts a bilingual AI town hall for families — materials in Spanish and English, childcare provided. Parents see live demonstrations of the vetted AI tools their children use, learn how student data is protected, and help co-create the district's AI use expectations. A parent committee is formed and meets quarterly. Trust — the scarcest resource in AI adoption — is built before a crisis creates it.

Outcome: The district reports 78% parent approval for AI-integrated learning, up from 31% two years earlier. Engagement precedes adoption.
Domain 2 · Student Standards

AI Literacy Across Every Subject

In a rural East Texas school district, revised TEKS require every content teacher — from 3rd-grade science to high school history — to integrate one AI literacy benchmark per semester. A 7th-grade social studies teacher uses AI to help students analyze primary sources, identify bias in AI-generated summaries of historical events, and then write their own corrected accounts. "Does this AI add value to the learning objective?" becomes the question every lesson plan must answer.

Outcome: AI literacy is no longer a CTE elective for 3,000 students. It is the baseline for 5.2 million.
Domain 4 · Procurement & Policy

The District That Didn't Have to Start from Scratch

A suburban superintendent receives TEA's model AI policy suite — student policy, staff policy, updated code of conduct, procurement checklist, and three district vignettes including one from a demographically similar district. Her board adopts a locally adapted version in a single meeting. Within 60 days, every teacher has a one-page guidance card, every parent has received an AI use notification in English and Spanish, and the district has completed a vendor vetting review.

Outcome: The 80% of districts that had no policy now have a clear, compliant, locally-owned starting point.
Domain 8 · Equity & Access

The White Paper That Reached the Legislature

The Texas AI in Education Task Force white paper is submitted directly to the Public Education Committee's interim study on AI in classrooms — timed precisely to the legislature's own charge. The paper's equity section — showing that AI access is concentrated in 12% of districts — triggers targeted state funding for rural and high-poverty campuses. Device parity, connectivity, and teacher PD grants reach districts that had been watching from the sidelines.

Outcome: The gap between districts that can afford AI literacy and those that cannot begins to close — by policy design, not chance.
