Phase 1: Surface Learning

Acquire foundational facts, labels, and individual skills. This phase is about the "what" before the "why."

SOLO Level: Uni-structural & Multi-structural (Click to expand)
Uni-structural: Students focus on one relevant aspect of a task.
Multi-structural: Students identify several relevant aspects but they are treated independently (a "list" of facts).
Jigsaw Method [cite: 633] d = 0.92
Grade 3-6 Example: In a science unit on ecosystems, each student in a "home group" is assigned one component (Producers, Consumers, or Decomposers). They meet with other "experts" to learn their role's definition and facts, then return to teach their home group. [cite: 161, 163]
Direct Instruction [cite: 639] d = 0.56
Grade 3-6 Example: A 4th-grade teacher models how to use an area model for double-digit multiplication on the whiteboard, followed by guided practice where the class solves three problems together before working independently. [cite: 36, 44]
Spaced Practice [cite: 367] d = 0.62
Grade 3-6 Example: Instead of one 60-minute session on vocabulary, students practice new words for 10 minutes every Monday, Wednesday, and Friday over two weeks to ensure long-term retention. [cite: 365, 369]
Retrieval Practice [cite: 392] d = 0.49
Grade 3-6 Example: 5th graders start Social Studies with a "3-minute brain dump," writing down everything they remember about the causes of the American Revolution without looking at their notes. [cite: 387, 394]
Phase 2: Deep Learning

Relate ideas and find patterns. This phase focuses on comprehension, interaction, and building a mental schema.

SOLO Level: Relational (Click to expand)
Relational: Students integrate several parts into a coherent whole. They understand the relationship between different concepts and how they contribute to the big picture.
Jigsaw Method [cite: 644] d = 0.92
Grade 3-6 Example: After gathering facts in the surface phase, Jigsaw groups now compare how their assigned ecosystem components interact (e.g., how the absence of Decomposers would impact Producers), creating a relationship map. [cite: 164, 166]
Argumentation [cite: 645] d = 0.86
Grade 3-6 Example: After reading two articles on school uniforms, 6th-grade students use evidence to debate whether uniforms impact student behavior, requiring them to evaluate claims and logic. [cite: 512, 520]
Concept Mapping [cite: 254] d = 0.64
Grade 3-6 Example: Students use a digital tool like Coggle to create a web showing how the water cycle, local weather patterns, and geography are interconnected in their specific region. [cite: 240, 254]
Phase 3: Transfer Learning

Apply learning to new, unfamiliar situations. Students demonstrate flexible understanding by generating novel solutions.

SOLO Level: Extended Abstract (Click to expand)
Extended Abstract: Students generalize the integrated whole to a higher level of abstraction. They can apply the concept to a completely new domain or create new hypotheses.
Problem-Solving Teaching [cite: 655] d = 0.88
Grade 3-6 Example: Students are presented with a real-world problem: a local pond is experiencing a sudden fish die-off. They must apply their knowledge of ecosystems and water cycles to hypothesize a cause and propose a solution. [cite: 492, 500]
Transfer Strategies [cite: 657] d = 0.75
Grade 3-6 Example: A teacher asks students to explain how the "Checks and Balances" system in the U.S. government is similar to the rules of a team sport they play at recess. [cite: 189, 211]
